Cutting Quotation Time by 57.2% for 1,500+ Enterprise Logistics Clients

The quotation process was slow and Sales were losing deals. I found the bottleneck, designed a faster workflow, and helped cut quotation completion time by 57.2%.

Overview

The Short Version

Waresix is a B2B logistics platform connecting businesses with trucking, warehousing, and freight services across Indonesia, serving 1,500+ enterprise clients. Quotations ran through three teams in sequence — Sales secured orders, Supply gathered vendor prices, Pricing finalized — and every handoff added delay. Sales lost deals because they could not get prices to customers fast enough, and any fix had to preserve pricing accuracy and profitability controls.

Through job shadowing, interviews, and time-motion analysis, I found the biggest bottleneck: Supply kept redoing work because the system would not let them reuse pricing data that already existed. I presented two directions to stakeholders, and the team chose the bolder one — let Sales set prices directly from historical data, with an approval flow for risky quotes. After testing and iteration, quotation completion time dropped 57.2%, measured on a dashboard the PM team set up.

My role

Sole UX designer, working with a PM, UX researcher, engineering manager, and team leads from Sales, Supply, and Pricing. I handled research, journey mapping, solution design, prototyping, testing, and stakeholder alignment.

Research

Discovery: Finding the Bottleneck

I sat with Supply officers — the suspected bottleneck — watching them work through daily tasks, and ran a time-motion study to see where time was actually being lost. Four issues came up consistently:

1
Duplicate work on similar orders.Pricing data from previous orders existed in the system but was locked to the task it was created for. Every similar request meant gathering the same prices again from scratch.
2
Complex shipments slowed everything down.Some orders had special constraints (specific container types, unusual routes, multiple stops) that made it harder to find matching vendors. These took significantly longer to process.
3
Multiple vendors per order.Comparing prices meant contacting several vendors, waiting for responses, and compiling results — multiplying the time per request.
4
Slow vendor responses.Vendors took hours or days to reply with pricing, and the whole quotation stalled in the meantime.

The core insight: Supply spent most of their time redoing work whose results already existed in the system.

Mapping

Mapping the Journey

I mapped the full journey across Sales, Supply, and Pricing to see every repetitive task and dependency in one picture.

The map showed the real problem was the sequential handoffs themselves, not Supply's speed. With direct access to pricing data, Sales would not need to wait for Supply at all on orders where historical data existed.

Strategy

Two Possible Directions

I sketched two solutions and presented the trade-offs to stakeholders.

Option 1
Let Supply reuse past quotations
Supply duplicates pricing from previous orders and adjusts as needed. Safer and less work for Supply, but Sales still waits on every order.
Option 2 (chosen)
Let Sales set prices directly
Sales sets prices themselves from historical data; Supply only steps in when no data exists. Faster, but riskier — Sales might set unprofitable prices without realizing it.

The team chose Option 2, with a safeguard: quotes flagged as potentially unprofitable would automatically go to a manager for review before reaching the customer.

Sales resistance

Sales pushed back: instead of handing off and waiting, they were now being asked to look up pricing, set margins, and own the numbers. I made the case that more effort per order meant faster responses and more closed deals, and that the approval flow protected them from sending out bad pricing.

Once they saw in the prototype that a quote could go out in minutes instead of days, they came around.

Design

Low-Fidelity Prototype

I drafted a flow where Sales could check past prices, set margins, and request approvals. Stakeholder reviews raised two points:

  1. The new flow had to feel as simple as the old one, or it would just create a different slowdown.
  2. Profitable quotes should pass automatically; only risky ones needed manager approval.

With engineers, I worked through the constraints of linking legacy pricing data — what would be accessible and what happens when no data exists for a route. I then simplified the navigation, added automatic approval triggers based on profit thresholds, and designed clear step-by-step screens.

Design

High-Fidelity Prototype

The final design covered the full quotation flow:

Create Quotation and Order Requirements

A simplified entry screen for shipment details (customer, service type, routes), with price suggestions based on historical data.

Price Checking

Each line item showed cost, target price, margin, and a selling price field, with indicators flagging whether the pricing looked good or needed attention.

Request Approval

Unprofitable quotes (selling price below cost) were automatically flagged for manager approval. Sales could attach a note and supporting documents explaining the price.

Email Approval Flow

Managers received an email notification with the quotation details and a link to review and approve directly.

Auto-Generated Quotation Document

Approved quotes generated a formatted quotation document ready to send to the customer.

Usability Testing

Usability Testing

I tested the prototype in person with five Sales officers. Three points came up:

  1. "I need to see past prices." Quick access to what similar orders cost before, to set competitive but profitable prices.
  2. "I can't tell if this quote is profitable." The initial design needed clearer visual signals for profit and loss.
  3. "What if there's no past data?" New routes and vendors had no historical pricing; they needed a fallback.

What I changed after testing

  • Replaced arrow/icon indicators with color-coded profit/loss signals (green, yellow, red) on each line item.
  • Designed a Price History view (previous quotes for the same product and route) and a searchable Price Book (all vendor agreements and pricing, filterable).
  • Added a "Request Price" option that routes orders with no historical data to Supply the traditional way.
  • Added Trip Details and Documents sections (route map, timeline, load info, proof of delivery) as context for pricing decisions.

Results

Impact

Quotation completion time reduction
57.2%
The new flow let Sales handle most quotes directly, only involving Supply when no historical data was available. This cut out the biggest source of delay in the process.

I designed the solution, but the impact was a team effort: the PM defined metrics and the dashboard, engineers made historical pricing accessible, and all three teams adopted the new workflow.

Reflection

Reflection

What went well

  • Job shadowing beat interviews — the locked-pricing-data insight only became visible by watching the actual workflow.
  • Presenting both options with trade-offs, instead of recommending one, gave stakeholders ownership of the decision.
  • The approval flow was the safeguard that made the bolder option viable at all.
  • Price History, Price Book, and Trip Details were out of scope but testing showed Sales needed reference data to price confidently — so I designed them.

What I would do differently

  • Add real-time analytics after launchOngoing monitoring would surface new bottlenecks as they emerge.
  • Spend more time on change managementA structured rollout with training and champions would have made adoption smoother.
  • Explore automation furtherSteps like auto-filling prices for frequent routes could have been automated entirely.
