Redesigning an EdTech Mobile App: From ~4K to ~712K Certificates in One Year

Rumah Siap Kerja's app was confusing users at every step. I rebuilt the information architecture, simplified every core flow, and built a component system, helping the platform scale from ~8K to ~574K+ customers in one year.

Overview

The Short Version

Rumah Siap Kerja (RSK) is an Indonesian edtech platform where citizens take job-skills video courses paid with Kartu Prakerja, a government voucher program. Payment means redeeming a code bought on an external marketplace (Tokopedia, Bukalapak), not a standard checkout, and the program was about to scale up enrollment fast. If the experience stayed confusing, that incoming traffic would go to competing platforms.

Research showed users were motivated to learn but the app was cluttered: unclear payment buttons, a banner-heavy homepage, and a dashboard that buried purchased classes. I restructured the information architecture, simplified every core flow, and built a component system. From 2020 to 2021, customers grew from ~8K+ to ~574K+, redemptions from ~5K+ to ~459K+, and certificates from ~4K+ to ~712K+.

My role

Lead and sole UX designer, managing two design interns and working with PMs, engineers, marketing, and C-level stakeholders. I owned research, IA, interaction design, prototyping, and testing.

Research

Discovery: Understanding What Was Going Wrong

I ran two research tracks in parallel: analyzing customer service tickets, and phone interviews with both active users and users who registered but never bought anything. Tickets surfaced the top complaints (payment confusion, finding purchased classes, downloading certificates); interviews explained why. Most RSK users had low digital literacy, and many had never used an e-learning app.

I do not have exact per-step drop-off numbers, but the patterns were consistent enough to identify four clear friction points:

1
Registration felt too long.The sign-up form showed too many required fields at once. Several interviewees said they gave up halfway through.
2
Payment was where most users got stuck.The redemption code flow had multiple action buttons with unclear names, and users did not understand what to do at each step.
3
The dashboard did not show what users were looking for.After buying, users expected to see their class right away. Instead they landed on a banner-filled homepage, and finding a purchased class or certificate took multiple taps through unclear menus.
4
The learning experience gave no sense of progress.Lessons appeared as a flat list with no progress indicators or feedback. Users could not tell how far along they were or whether they passed.

Building the persona

From these findings I created Fani, a 25-year-old recent graduate from Bekasi: motivated to learn, inexperienced with digital products, quick to give up when something feels unclear. She became the filter for every design decision. If Fani would not understand something on her first try, it needed to be simpler.

Analysis

UX Audit: Mapping the Specific Issues

I ran a heuristic evaluation using Shneiderman's 8 Golden Rules, leaning on "Strive for Consistency" and "Reduce Short-Term Memory Load" — the rules that matter most for users with low digital literacy. The audit showed:

  • Buttons and interactions behaved differently on different screens
  • Promotional banners and sliders on the homepage competed with the actual navigation
  • Going from "I want to take a class" to enrolling took too many steps through confusing menus
  • Actions like buying a class gave no clear confirmation, so users were not sure it worked

Studying Ruangguru (Indonesia's largest edtech) and Udacity showed two things RSK was missing: screens focused on one decision at a time, and progress indicators (bars, badges, grades) that keep users engaged across sessions.

Strategy

The Strategic Decision: Structural Redesign Over Quick Fixes

At this point the project could have gone two ways.

Option A
Small fixes
Improve button labels, clarify payment instructions, redesign a few cards. Lower risk, faster to ship, but the underlying structure was the problem.
Option B (chosen)
Full structural redesign
Rebuild the information architecture, restructure every core flow, simplify aggressively. More work and risk, but it addressed the root cause.

I pushed for Option B: research showed problems in registration, payment, discovery, learning, and certification, not just one step. The goal became reducing every core flow — sign up, find a class, buy it, learn, get the certificate — to as few steps as possible.

The disagreement about banners

The C-level and marketing team believed more promotional banners on the homepage meant more sales. I argued the opposite: users arrived with a clear goal (find a class, buy it) and the clutter was driving them away — and the ticket data backed that up.

The evidence won buy-in for a cleaner homepage where promotions no longer compete with core navigation, and we agreed to measure the impact after launch.

Architecture

Information Architecture: Rebuilding the Structure

The old navigation scattered courses, categories, settings, transactions, and support across layers that had grown without a plan. I rebuilt the IA around three user goals:

  1. Find classes: Home + Catalog
  2. Learn: Class player + content
  3. Track progress: Dashboard with classes, grades, and certificates

Everything else (settings, transaction history, help center) moved to a side menu; the bottom tab bar served only the three primary goals. I considered a fourth "Payment" tab but rejected it — payment is a step within buying a class, not a goal, and it needed to feel like the natural next step after selecting one.

Design

Design Solutions: Connected to Research Findings

Each design change came directly from something I found in research.

Registration: fewer fields + social sign-in

I made Google sign-in the primary option and cut manual registration to four fields (name, email, phone, password). Helper text explained upfront that the name must match government records, instead of letting users fail later at payment.

Catalog and search: category chips + unified search

I replaced the cluttered homepage grid with a clean catalog: category filter chips, a search covering both classes and categories, and sort options (rating, newest, price).

Class details: sticky buy button + clear syllabus

Users were unsure what a class included before buying. The new detail page kept the purchase button always visible, put learning outcomes near the top, and added an expandable syllabus with module names and durations.

Payment: default to Kartu Prakerja + simplified flow

Since almost everyone paid with Kartu Prakerja, I made it the default and replaced the confusing action buttons with a single voucher-code input plus short explanation text. Other methods sat behind a "Change Method" button.

Dashboard: My Class tab

"My Class" became the default dashboard tab, with certificates accessible right from the completed class card and a grade chart showing scores against the passing threshold.

Learning experience: playlist structure + quizzes

I restructured course content into a playlist-style player with collapsible modules, durations, and completed/current indicators. Quizzes between modules gave immediate feedback, and a workbook/essay section added hands-on practice.

Help center: in-app Kartu Prakerja guide

Support was overwhelmed with Kartu Prakerja questions, so I built an in-app help center with step-by-step visual guides for registering and redeeming vouchers — cutting repeat tickets and heading off payment confusion before it happened.

Usability Testing

Testing and Iteration

I ran two rounds of remote usability testing through Maze with first-time digital learners matching the persona.

Round 1 results:

Scenario Completion Rate
Create an RSK account 100% (5/5)
Find a specific class ("Menanam Hidroponik") 80% (4/5)
Buy a class using Kartu Prakerja 80% (4/5)
Enter the class and start learning 80% (4/5)

Three issues came up:

  1. Users could not tell which classes were Kartu Prakerja-eligible without tapping into each one.
  2. Users struggled to find where to buy their redemption code — nothing pointed them to the external marketplace.
  3. Users expected certificates directly on the dashboard, without extra navigation.

What I changed after Round 1

  • Added a visible "Prakerja" badge on eligible course cards in the catalog.
  • Added Active/Completed filter tabs so completed classes (with certificate downloads) were immediately accessible.
  • Added direct marketplace links (Tokopedia, Bukalapak, etc.) on the payment screen so users could buy the code and come back.

Round 2 testing confirmed these fixes worked. All scenarios reached 100% completion.

System

Design System Contribution

I built foundational patterns that became the standard for RSK's product going forward:

  • Component library in FigmaButtons, form fields, cards, navigation, and modals, each with all states (default, hover, active, disabled, error).
  • Color and typography tokensPrimary blue, semantic colors for success/error/warning, and a clear type scale.
  • Spacing and layout patterns8px grid, standardized card sizes, predictable padding.
  • Documented interaction patternsBottom-sheet modals, filter chips, tab navigation, and persistent action buttons.

With documented patterns, my two interns could build matching screens without me reviewing every detail, and engineers could reference the library instead of guessing from mockups.

Team

Mentorship

I set up my two interns' work so they learned while contributing:

  • Ownership of specific screens within the design system — real work, not exercises.
  • Regular critique sessions where I explained my reasoning, including what I considered and rejected.
  • The persona as our shared quality bar: "Would Fani understand this?"

It was my first time managing designers, and it taught me how to scale design quality beyond my own output.

Reflection

Reflection

What went well

  • "As few steps as possible" kept decisions focused — every proposed feature or screen had to justify itself.
  • A persona built from real interviews replaced opinion debates with "what would Fani do?"
  • The best fixes (Prakerja as default payment, marketplace links) came directly from testing, not the initial design.

What I would do differently

  • Set up analytics tracking from day oneWe launched without event tracking, so the impact numbers are platform-level totals rather than per-step funnel data.
  • Test with a wider range of users earlierOur participants were mostly urban smartphone users; testing with rural users on older phones would have caught performance and layout issues sooner.
  • Build a more complete design systemWith more time I would have added usage guidelines, do/don't examples, and accessibility notes per component.
